# Bio-Inspired Event-Based Visual Servoing for Ground Robots 

Maral Mordad 1,∗, Kian Behzad 1,∗, Debojyoti Biswas 2, Noah J. Cowan 2,3,†, and Milad Siami 1,†

Abstract — Biological sensory systems are inherently adaptive, filtering out constant stimuli and prioritizing relative changes, likely enhancing computational and metabolic efficiency. In-spired by active sensing behaviors across a wide range of animals, this paper presents a novel event-based visual servoing framework for ground robots. Utilizing a Dynamic Vision Sensor (DVS), we demonstrate that by applying a fixed spa-tial kernel to the asynchronous event stream generated from structured logarithmic intensity-change patterns, the resulting net event flux analytically isolates specific kinematic states. We establish a generalized theoretical bound for this event rate estimator and show that linear and quadratic spatial profiles isolate the robot’s velocity and position-velocity product, respec-tively. Leveraging these properties, we employ a multi-pattern stimulus to directly synthesize a nonlinear state-feedback term entirely without traditional state estimation. To overcome the inescapable loss of linear observability at equilibrium inherent in event sensing, we propose a bio-inspired active sensing limit-cycle controller. Experimental validation on a 1/10-scale autonomous ground vehicle confirms the efficacy, extreme low-latency, and computational efficiency of the proposed direct-sensing approach. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Biological sensors function primarily as relative detectors that attenuate constant (i.e., low-frequency or “DC”) stim-uli while emphasizing changes (“AC”) [1], a characteristic known as sensory adaptation. To prevent stationary objects from becoming “invisible” due to these high-pass sensors, animals execute continuous bursts of back-and-forth swim-ming motions [2]–[4] that convert static spatial gradients into detectable temporal signals [5]. Examples include electric fish [2], [3], [6], rodents actively whisking to induce friction-based micro-accelerations [7], insects relying on flight-driven optic flow [1], blind cave fish gliding to distort pressure waves [8], and the human visual system employing invol-untary microsaccades to prevent perceptual fading [9]. Such adaptive, high-pass sensing, while highly efficient and low-latency, poses significant design challenges for robotics. For example, in mobile robotics, conventional state estimation and control methods fail under these conditions, as proved in [10]. Traditional sensors, such as frame-based cameras, IMUs, and LiDAR, rely on power-hungry, absolute sensing. They capture data at fixed intervals regardless of

For this reason, replicat-ing bio-inspired principles is crucial for future developments, which requires a fundamental shift from these traditional methods to relative, event-driven sensing [11]–[13]. In this work, we employ a neuromorphic event camera, also known as a Dynamic Vision Sensor (DVS). Serving as a technological analog to biological vision, the DVS samples light based on scene dynamics rather than an arbitrary exter-nal clock [13]. Instead of capturing full images at fixed frame rates, each pixel operates independently and asynchronously. A pixel triggers a spike (an address-event) only when its local logarithmic brightness changes by a preset threshold, producing a continuous stream of event tuples containing the pixel coordinates, a microsecond-resolution timestamp, and the polarity of the change (brightness increase or decrease) [14], [15]. Consequently, the DVS offers several key advan-tages over standard cameras, including microsecond temporal resolution, high dynamic range (HDR), and immunity to mo-tion blur [13], [16]. Furthermore, by transmitting data exclu-sively during motion, the DVS performs inherent hardware-level compression, effectively filtering out redundant static backgrounds. Motivated by these biological principles, we aim to de-velop robust Event-Based Visual Servoing (EBVS) for em-bodied intelligent agents, such as ground robots, drones, and collaborative factory robots. EBVS entails moving either an event camera or the camera’s visual target such that the generated event stream drives the camera to a desired region [17]. Image-Based Visual Servoing (IBVS) is widely estab-lished in the literature, traditionally relying on frame-based cameras to extract and match geometric features against a stored “desired” view [14], [18], [19]. Kernel-Based Vi-sual Servoing (KBVS) [20], [21] bypasses explicit feature extraction and segmentation by applying a spatial kernel that processes the entire image as a continuous signal. This kernel-based paradigm serves as the foundational framework for our proposed approach to EBVS. To reduce computa-tional overhead, Garcia et al. [22] emulated an event-based camera to trigger IBVS updates only when tracked features crossed spatial boundaries. Similarly, Gil et al. [23] utilized event cameras for high-speed feature tracking, falling back to frame-based vision when necessary. Pushing toward fully event-driven perception, Muthusamy et al. [17] replaced con-ventional vision processing with event-based feature tracking while retaining traditional IBVS control laws. More recently, learning-based approaches have emerged; for example, Vinod et al. [24] employed deep neural networks to map fused RGB images and simulated event streams directly to motor

commands. In this work, we propose a purely event-driven and computationally efficient EBVS framework that applies a spatial kernel to predefined intensity patterns. The objective is to regulate the camera position to a desired region of the displayed pattern. Our approach avoids explicit feature extraction, feature tracking, and state estimation. In fact, it relies solely on the net number of events generated by the event camera, leading to a highly computationally effi-cient implementation. To maintain system observability and overcome sensory adaptation, we adapt the control-theoretic framework proposed in [10]. This allows us to formulate a direct sensor-to-motor active sensing control law with proven stability conditions. Our overall contribution is a novel EBVS approach that overcomes the lack of local observability inherent in event-based sensing using a bio-inspired, limit-cycle-based control law with guaranteed (local) exponential convergence 1.II. C. Synthesis of Event-Driven Feedback 

With the robot’s longitudinal dynamics established in (6), the core control challenge is to regulate this system utilizing only the asynchronous event streams modeled in Section II-A. Conventional state observers are ill-suited for this task; formulating continuous-time estimators for asynchronous, relative neuromorphic data is highly complex, and more critically, the plant fundamentally loses local observability as the camera velocity approaches zero. Rather than attempting explicit state reconstruction, we leverage a dual-pattern vi-sual stimulus. By concurrently computing the event flux over a quadratic kernel ( K1) and a linear kernel ( K2), we indepen-dently estimate the position-velocity product x(t) ˙ x(t) via (4) and the instantaneous velocity ˙x(t) via (5). Multiplying these parallel streams allows us to directly synthesize the higher-order nonlinear feedback term, x(t) ˙ x(t)2, entirely within the sensor domain. The subsequent section details how this synthesized signal is embedded into a bio-inspired active sensing control law to actively overcome these fundamental observability limitations. III. This paper presented a purely event-driven, bio-inspired visual servoing framework that bypasses computationally expensive feature extraction by directly coupling spatial intensity patterns with the net event rate of a dynamic vision sensor. By demonstrating that specific spatial profiles isolate distinct kinematic states, we successfully synthesized direct nonlinear state feedback and maintained system observability at equilibrium using a limit-cycle active sensing controller. Future work will focus on extending this framework to multi-dimensional visual servoing and exploring its robustness against dynamic targets in unstructured environments.
