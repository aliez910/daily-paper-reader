Abstract — Collaborative transport requires robots to infer partner intent through physical interaction while maintaining stable loco-manipulation. This becomes particularly challenging in complex environments, where interaction signals are difficult to capture and model. We present PAINT, a lightweight yet efficient hierarchical learning framework for partner-agonistic intent-aware collaborative legged transport that infers partner intent directly from proprioceptive feedback. PAINT decouples intent understanding from terrain-robust locomotion: A high-level policy infers the partner interaction wrench using an intent estimator and a teacher-student training scheme, while a low-level locomotion backbone ensures robust execution. This enables lightweight deployment without external force-torque sensing or payload tracking. Extensive simulation and real-world experiments demonstrate compliant cooperative trans-port across diverse terrains, payloads, and partners. I. I NTRODUCTION 

Collaborative transport in heterogeneous teams of humans and robots is an important topic with applications in fac-tory assistance and field operations. Collaborative robots can substantially reduce a partner’s physical workload by assisting in the transport of large and heavy payloads [1]. In practical collaborative transport, one partner typically acts as the leader, initiating the desired motion of the payload, while the collaborator follows by interpreting the leader’s mo-tion intent. Effective collaboration requires follower robots to maintain robust, terrain-aware locomotion under load while simultaneously inferring the partner’s motion intent to achieve smooth, compliant transport [2]. To be viable for real-world applications across diverse scenarios, the robot should be able to collaborate with heterogeneous partners, including humans and robots, across varying team sizes. 

> arXiv:2604.12852v1 [cs.RO] 14 Apr 2026

In this work, we aim to develop a control pipeline that enables a robot to interpret and follow a leader’s motion intent during collaborative transport. Motion intent is rep-resented by the force and torque that the leader applies to the payload to guide the transport movement [3], [4], [5]. The leading partner may be either a human or another robot, and the transport team may include additional heterogeneous robots. Our goal is therefore to enable partner-agnostic col-laboration during transport, inferring motion intent from the leading partner, while maintaining robust locomotion over uneven terrains. In addition, to minimize system overhead for practical applications, such as factories and construction sites, the pipeline must be lightweight and easily deployable. However, achieving this goal remains challenging for sev-eral reasons. First, motion intent is hard to capture and model across diverse environments with heterogeneous human-robot teams. For this reason, many intent-aware transport systems rely on external sensors, such as Force-Torque (FT) sensors, to estimate interaction intent [3], [4], [5]. Although informative, such sensors are often too expensive and fragile for high-impact, high-wear deployment on factory floors, and are susceptible to locomotion-induced noise that is hard to filter out. Second, most existing intent-aware frameworks are validated only on flat, structured laboratory terrain [6], [7], [8]. Their robustness on uneven terrain remains insufficiently explored, where the robot must maintain stable locomotion while correctly responding to the partner’s intent. Finally, most previous studies focus on single human-robot transport pairs, while extending such interaction mechanisms to larger teams remains less explored. Existing multi-robot transport systems often rely on motion capture infrastructure [9], remote control [10], or predefined team configurations [11], [12]. Such assumptions limit portability to infrastructure-free deployment, particularly in outdoor or field settings, and make it harder to team with a flexible number of robots or heterogeneous partners. To this end, we propose PAINT , a lightweight yet effi-cient hierarchical learning framework for Partner-Agnostic 

INT ent-aware cooperative transport using only accessible joint-level proprioceptive signals. While PAINT is robot-agnostic and applies to other morphologies, we specifically focus on quadrupedal robots for their robust locomotion on uneven terrains [13], [14]. PAINT decouples intent under-standing from terrain-robust locomotion, where a High-Level (HL) transport policy infers partner intent from propriocep-tive histories without FT sensing or payload tracking, and a Low-Level (LL) locomotion backbone tracks base motion commands and stabilizes the robot in diverse uneven terrains. We adopt a teacher-student learning scheme to make the learned HL policy deployable under partial observability: a privileged teacher is trained with ground-truth interaction wrench data, while a student learns to reconstruct the in-teraction wrench via an intent estimator network from pro-prioception [15], [16]. PAINT can be zero-shot deployed to collaborative transport systems with team-member-agnostic coordination interface. When multiple robots interact through a shared payload, their collective behavior is facilitated by payload-coupled signals. This allows the team-level intent-aware interface to extend naturally to decentralized multi-robot transport and to transfer across robot embodiments by swapping the locomotion backbone. The contributions of this work are as follows.  

> •

A lightweight yet efficient hierarchical learning frame-work. We introduce a hierarchical framework that de-couples partner-intent inference from terrain-robust loco-motion, which simplifies policy learning across diverse environments. By leveraging a pretrained LL locomotion backbone, the HL policy can focus on generating transport behaviors aligned with partner motion intent, making the overall pipeline lightweight, efficient, and scalable.  

> •

Intent-aware HL policy. We design an intent estimator and a teacher-student training pipeline for HL policy to infer partner motion intent purely from joint-level proprio-ceptive signals, enabling deployment with diverse partners and payloads without FT sensors at the end-effector or explicit payload tracking.  

> •

Scalability to decentralized multi-robot transport. We show that the learned intent-aware formulation is not only partner-agnostic but can also be zero-shot transferred to multi-robot teams of flexible sizes. Through extensive sim-ulation and real-world experiments, we provide evidence that pure proprioceptive signals from payload-coupled interaction provide a scalable interface for decentralized team transport. II. R ELATED WORK 

Cooperative transport is a classical case of Human-Robot Collaboration (HRC). Early work emphasizes compliance via impedance-admittance regulation, enabling intuitive leader-follower behaviors and safe assistance [17]. Recent ad-vancements are extending classical compliant cooperative transport toward intent-aware cooperative transport [3], [4], [5], [6]. Existing intent-aware approaches broadly follow two directions. Model-based methods achieve robustness through constraint-aware reasoning and structured inference [3], [6], [7], [8], [18]. They are effective under reliable state observability, but depend on accurate modeling. In contrast, learning-based intent encoding can better tolerate contact variability, adapt to complex payload-coupled dy-namics, and enable richer coordination behaviors [2], [4], [10], [12]. However, several systems still rely on FT sensing [3], [4], [5], [19], which improves observability but increases deployment overhead due to cost and fragility. This has motivated lightweight alternatives that infer partner intent with only joint states and body dynamics [2], [6], without FT sensors at the end-effector. Beyond the sensing modality, intent representation also matters. Explicit representation [20] makes the inferred intent physically meaningful and improves human interpretability. It also makes the intent estimator easy to modularize for downstream control. In addition, cooperative transport can build on progress in legged loco-manipulation. Whole-body frameworks [15], [21] are widely adopted for coordinating the robot’s arm and legs. However, this end-to-end control framework prevents reuse of established locomotion backbones [13], [14], [22] across diverse terrains, payloads, and partners, thereby requiring costly and time-consuming retraining. Instead, hierarchical designs can offer a simpler and more practical integration pathway [23], [20]. Beyond single-robot HRC, multi-robot cooperative trans-port introduces additional sensing and coordination chal-lenges. Prior work stabilizes collaboration via centralized tracking and planning [12], motion capture [9], remote control [10], or fixed team configurations [11], [12], [24], limiting scalability and field deployment. In addition, limited attention has been paid to inferring the team-level intent from physical payload-coupled interactions during multi-robot co-operative transport [2]. Our work targets an explicit intent representation learned from proprioceptive histories from payload-coupled physical interactions during collaborative transport, designed to pair with terrain-robust locomotion backbones and to naturally extend to team-level intent-aware coordination in multi-robot transport. C. Intent Estimator in HL Student Policy 

A key challenge in deployable intent-aware collaborative transport is that the interaction wrench at the payload is typically unobservable. Although FT sensors can measure interaction wrenches, they are not always available for high-impact, high-wear deployment in practice. Instead, time-correlated joint proprioceptive signals are real-world accessi-ble observations and can be used to infer partner-applied FT during payload-coupled interaction. Therefore, we augment the student HL policy with an intent estimator network that estimates the interaction wrench from proprioceptive histo-ries. The predicted wrench is then fed back into the policy as part of its observation. This estimator can be interpreted as learning an inverse observation kernel: while the observation kernel Ω maps the latent state st to the observation ot, the estimator infers latent intent variables from the observed proprioceptive signals induced by interaction.

We train our policies in Isaac Gym using 4096 parallel environments on a single RTX 3080Ti GPU. We additionally randomize the payload mass to avoid overfitting to a single payload-dynamics regime, sampling the mass uniformly from 

0 kg to 10 kg . The LL policy follows the setup in [23]. For the HL policy, both the teacher and the student use three-layer MLP actor and critic networks with hidden dimensions (128, 128, 128). The intent estimator network adopts the same MLP architecture. The teacher and student HL policies are trained for 15k and 20k iterations. To further increase policy robustness and reduce sim-to-real gaps, domain randomiza-tion is applied to the robot’s proprioceptive observations and the generated interactive wrench. V. E XPERIMENTS AND RESULTS 

A. Real-World Transport Across Diverse Situations 

Fig. 3a demonstrates that the robot can reliably infer part-ner intent using only arm proprioceptive histories, without requiring FT sensors or payload tracking. Across uneven terrains, such as stairs and gravel, the learned HL policy produces smooth planar base commands that the LL locomo-tion backbone tracks robustly, resulting in stable co-carrying despite terrain-induced perturbations. Crucially, the method remains effective across variations in payload geometries and weights, indicating that the inferred intent is not overfitted to a single payload configuration. Finally, cooperative transport remains stable when partnering with different leaders, such as human and humanoid, supporting the claim that the proposed pipeline is partner-agnostic. 

B. Quantitative Studies Across Different Payload Masses 

Table II reports the single-agent quantitative results under different payload masses and the same interaction force and torque in simulation. The compared baselines include Damped Arm, Pure RL-4 [27], BC Distillation-4 [2], and Force Estimator-4 [15], [16]. Damped Arm is implemented via classical PD control: the arm is in damping mode, and the end-effector displacement induced by external forces and torques is passed to the PD controller to compute base mo-tion commands. A low-pass filter is used to improve motion smoothness. All other methods in baseline comparisons use 4-step ( 0.08 s ) proprioception histories, while in ablation studies, different history steps are compared. As payload mass increases, all methods generally degrade, suggesting that heavier loads induce more challenging coupled dynam-ics. Across masses, PAINT remains the most robust method, consistently achieving the best or near-best metrics. Notably, although Force Est.-4 achieves competitive FT estimation accuracy, its tracking errors are typically higher than those of PAINT-4, suggesting that PAINT benefits not only from informative interaction wrenches but also from the teacher-student distillation. The end-effector metrics further support this trend, with PAINT generally producing lower constraint FT and stronger intent alignment than the baselines. C. Scaling to Multi-Robot Teams 

Fig. 3b evaluates multi-robot scalability by deploying the same transport policy on teams of 2-4 legged robots trans-porting large and heavy payloads in simulation and the real world. The multi-robot teams successfully transport various payloads, including a sofa, a desk, and a barrel, over uneven terrain while maintaining coherent motions in simulation, and jointly carry an 8 kg long box with a human leader in the real world. Fig. 5 shows the improved performance of multi-robot teams when transporting heavy payloads. In the multi-robot setting, PAINT maintains high intent alignment across payloads up to 28 kg . Although trained in a single-agent setting with up to 10 kg payload, PAINT remains effective under challenging payload-coupled dynamics with higher payload masses and team sizes unseen from training. Notably, this coordination remains fully decentralized, with each robot running the same policy and inferring the team-level intent solely from its individual proprioceptive histories, without centralized planning or explicit state sharing. 

D. Scaling to Different Robot Embodiments 

In this experiment, we transfer PAINT to ANYmal without an arm. For the new robot embodiment, the HL policy takes proprioceptive signals from leg joints as input and outputs base motion commands for its LL locomotion backbone. F. Failure Cases and Limitations 

The proposed framework has limitations in environments requiring active obstacle avoidance. As shown in Fig. 7, when encountering obstacles such as walls or narrow struc-tures, the HL policy lacks explicit perception or planning ca-pabilities to proactively avoid collisions. Instead, it passively follows the leader’s motion, sending unsafe base commands to the LL locomotion backbone, which can cause locomotion failure when the leading partner does not provide sufficient corrective guidance. VI. C ONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK 

We present PAINT, a lightweight yet efficient partner-agnostic intent-aware framework for cooperative transport with legged robots. By combining a terrain-robust LL lo-comotion backbone with a HL transport policy that infers partner intent purely from joint-level proprioceptive histories, our approach enables lightweight deployment without FT sensors or payload tracking. Extensive simulation and real-world experiments demonstrate that the proposed frame-work supports stable cooperative transport across diverse environments, and generalizes across different payloads, team compositions, and robot embodiments. Furthermore, we validate that proprioceptive signals induced by payload-coupled interactions can encode meaningful interaction cues, overcoming partial observability in both robot-leader and multi-robot transport teams. Several directions remain for future work. First, partner intent is represented by a planar wrench, which is not suffi-cient for more complex environments. Full 6D interaction wrenches and more semantic-aware intent representations can be explored. Second, the current HL policy allows only limited arm motion. Future research can enable the arm to move more freely, thus improving the ability to perform payload reorientation and handle constrained maneuvers, such as turning in narrow corridors. Lastly, collaborative grasping and lifting can be incorporated to make the pipeline more complete and practical for payload transportation. VII. A CKNOWLEDGEMENT
