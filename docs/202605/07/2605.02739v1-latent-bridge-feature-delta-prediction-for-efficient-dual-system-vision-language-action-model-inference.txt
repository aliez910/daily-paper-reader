## Abstract 

Dual-system Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models achieve state-of-the-art robotic manipulation but are bottlenecked by the VLM backbone, which must execute at every control step while producing temporally redundant features. We propose Latent Bridge , a lightweight model that predicts VLM output deltas be-tween timesteps, enabling the action head to operate on predicted outputs while the expensive VLM backbone is called only periodically. We instantiate Latent Bridge on two architecturally distinct VLAs: GR00T-N1.6 (feature-space bridge) and 

ПЂ0.5 (KV-cache bridge), demonstrating that the approach generalizes across VLA designs. Our task-agnostic DAgger training pipeline transfers across benchmarks without modification. Across four LIBERO suites, 24 RoboCasa kitchen tasks, and the ALOHA sim transfer-cube task, Latent Bridge achieves 95вЂ“100% perfor-mance retention while reducing VLM calls by 50вЂ“75% , yielding 1.65вЂ“1.73 Г— net per-episode speedup . Our code and checkpoints are publicly available 1.

## 1 Introduction 

Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models have emerged as a promising paradigm for generalist robotic manipulation, leveraging pretrained vision-language models to ground language instructions in visual observations and produce motor commands Brohan et al. [2023b], Black et al. [2024], Bjorck et al. [2025]. Among VLA architectures, dual-system designsвЂ”where a large VLM backbone produces a feature representation that a separate, lightweight action head decodes into actionsвЂ”have achieved state-of-the-art results on diverse manipulation benchmarks. Unlike single-system VLAs that autoregressively generate action tokens through the LLM itself (e.g., RT-2 Brohan et al. [2023b], OpenVLA Kim et al. [2024]), dual-system VLAs (e.g., GR00T Bjorck et al. [2025], ПЂ0.5 Physical Intelligence et al. [2025], Octo Ghosh et al. [2024]) decompose inference into: 1. Stage 1 (VLM backbone): A vision encoder + language model processes the observation 

ot to produce features zt в€€ RN Г—D . This is the heavyweight component and dominates inference time. 2. Stage 2 (Action head): A lightweight policy network decodes zt into action at, running significantly faster than Stage 1. This decomposition creates a fundamental asymmetric latency bottleneck : the VLM backbone must execute at every control step, yet produces outputs that change slowly over time. For flow-matching action heads (GR00TвЂ™s DiT Bjorck et al. [2025], ПЂ0.5вЂ™s flow expert Physical Intelligence et al. [2025])вЂ”both sample the time variable from a Beta(1 .5, 1.0) distribution biased toward the data sideвЂ”reducing denoising from в‰Ґ10 steps to a single step incurs negligible performance loss in our experiments, making the VLM backbone the clear bottleneck. For real-time manipulation at 10вЂ“50Hz, this severely limits deployment. 

Key insight: Feature temporal redundancy. We observe that consecutive VLM outputs exhibit high temporal similarity, meaning each expensive backbone call produces features that are largely redundant with the previous timestep. This redundancy suggests that a much smaller model could 

predict the output delta в€†t = zt+1 в€’ zt, allowing the action head to operate on predicted features while the VLM backbone is called only every f steps. 

Our approach. We propose Latent Bridge , a lightweight model that predicts one-step VLM output deltas: 

Л†zt+1 = Л† zt + B(Л† zt, s t, q t, a tв€’1) (1) where Л†zt is the most recent representation (the fresh VLM feature on a VLM step, or the bridgeвЂ™s own previous prediction on a chained bridge step), st provides observation-dependent context (stable intermediate features or fresh vision embeddings), and qt, atв€’1 are the robot state and previous action. Between periodic VLM calls (every f = 2 вЂ“4 steps), the bridge chains predictions autoregressively, replacing the VLM backbone at orders-of-magnitude lower latency. A bridge trained on sync data aloneвЂ”rollouts collected with the VLM running every step, so the bridge always sees clean VLM features as inputsвЂ”learns from a clean-input distribution, but at deployment it must consume its own predictions during autoregressive chaining, creating a distribution shift that sync-only training does not cover. We close this gap with a task-agnostic DAgger pipeline : the bridge is rolled out in the simulator while the VLM runs in parallel to provide ground-truth feature targets along the bridgeвЂ™s own trajectory, and the bridge is retrained on this mixed distribution. This pipeline transfers across all four LIBERO task suites without modification. Our full pipeline therefore consists of three stages applied task-adaptively: Stage 1 is the bridge learning stage (sync collection + R0 supervised training + DAgger R1 refinement) and is the only stage required for simpler benchmarks; Stage 2 (LoRA action-head adaptation) and Stage 3 (phase-aware VLM scheduling) are added for long-horizon tasks where autoregressive bridge drift compounds over longer rollouts. Our contributions are: 1. Latent Bridge : a lightweight delta predictor that skips >50% of VLM backbone calls on intermediate steps; orthogonal to and composable with existing VLM acceleration methods (pruning, quantization, layer skipping). 22. We instantiate the bridge on two architecturally distinct VLAsвЂ”feature-space for GR00T and KV-cache for ПЂ0.5вЂ”and evaluate on LIBERO (4 suites), RoboCasa (24 kitchen tasks), and ALOHA sim (transfer-cube), achieving 95вЂ“100% SR retention with 1.65вЂ“1.73 Г— net speedup .3. A complete three-stage pipeline (bridge learning; LoRA action-head adaptation; phase-aware scheduling), applied task-adaptivelyвЂ”Stage 1 alone recovers near-sync performance on shorter-horizon benchmarks, and all three stages together fully recover synchronous VLA performance on long-horizon tasksвЂ”trained on a single GPU in a few hours. 

## 2 Related Work 

Vision-Language-Action models. VLA models combine pretrained vision-language understanding with action prediction for robotic control. Single-system VLAsвЂ”RT-1 Brohan et al. [2023a], RT-2 Brohan et al. [2023b], OpenVLA Kim et al. [2024], and RT-X Padalkar et al. [2024]вЂ”tokenize actions and generate them autoregressively through the LLM, inheriting its sequential decoding bottleneck. Dual-system VLAs decouple perception from action generation: GR00T Bjorck et al. [2025] uses a DiT action head, ПЂ0.5 Physical Intelligence et al. [2025] uses an interleaved flow-matching expert, Octo Ghosh et al. [2024] and HPT Wang et al. [2024] use diffusion decoders, and CogACT Li et al. [2024a] uses a continuous action decoder. Diffusion Policy Chi et al. [2023] demonstrates that diffusion-based action heads achieve strong multi-modal behavior cloning. Our work targets the dual-system architecture, exploiting the separable representation between backbone and action head to skip backbone computation on intermediate steps. 

Efficient VLM/VLA inference. General VLM acceleration techniques include KV cache com-pression Li et al. [2024b], Xiao et al. [2024], speculative decoding Leviathan et al. [2023], Cai et al. [2024], visual token pruning Chen et al. [2024], Bolya et al. [2023], and weight quantization Lin et al. [2024], Frantar et al. [2023]. VLA-specific adaptations span token pruning (VLA-Cache Xu et al. [2025], SpecPrune-VLA Wang et al. [2025], LightVLA Jiang et al. [2026], VLA-Pruner Liu et al. [2025], Compressor-VLA Gao et al. [2025]), layer skipping (MoLe-VLA Zhang et al. [2026]), and quantization (SQAP-VLA Fang et al. [2025], DyQ-VLA Zheng et al. [2026]), reporting 1.08вЂ“1.62 Г—

per-step speedups with various SR trade-offs. None measure net episode wall-clock time, and all still execute the full backboneвЂ”making them complementary to, not competitive with, our approach: rather than optimizing the VLM forward pass, we skip it entirely on bridge steps, and the bridge composes with any of the above on VLM steps that do execute. 

World models and latent dynamics. World models learn environment dynamics for planning or data augmentation. Ha & Schmidhuber Ha and Schmidhuber [2018] learn spatial (VAE) and temporal (RNN) representations; the Dreamer line Hafner et al. [2020, 2023] trains policies via backpropagation through latent imagination; IRIS Micheli et al. [2023] uses a discrete autoregressive world model with a transformer. In the visual domain, UniSim Yang et al. [2024] generates realistic experiences via a generative model conditioned on diverse actions, and Genie Bruce et al. [2024] produces controllable environments from internet video. LeCunвЂ™s JEPA framework LeCun [2022] and V-JEPA Bardes et al. [2024] advocate prediction in representation space rather than pixel space, which is closest to our bridgeвЂ™s operating principle. Our bridge similarly predicts future latent states, but in the VLMвЂ™s hidden representation space rather than a learned abstract state, and serves to accelerate inference rather than enable planning or representation learning.

Baselines. We compare against FastV Chen et al. [2024] and VLA-Cache Xu et al. [2025] as token pruning baselines, along with feature caching ( в€†t=0 ). The majority of VLA pruning methods (VLA-Cache, LightVLA, VLA-Pruner, Compressor-VLA, MoLe-VLA, etc.) are designed and evaluated exclusively on single-system VLAs such as OpenVLA, where the LLM backbone directly generates action tokens. While SpecPrune-VLA Wang et al. [2025] reports results on ПЂ0.5, it is not open-sourced and involves extensive hyperparameter tuning (action-aware pruning thresholds, layer selection), making faithful reproduction difficult. In dual-system VLAs, the diffusion or flow-matching action head is sensitive to the dense conditioning provided by VLM hidden states or KV caches, making token pruning adaptation considerably harder than in single-system VLAs where such techniques are already mature. We select FastV and VLA-Cache as they are open-sourced and reproducible, and we adapt them to dual-system VLAs (GR00T and ПЂ0.5) for fair comparison.

## H Baseline Implementation Details 

Token-pruning baselines (FastV, VLA-Cache) were originally designed and evaluated on single-system VLAs (e.g., OpenVLA) where the LLM backbone autoregressively generates action tokens. We re-implemented both from their official repositories and adapted them to the dual-system archi-tectures studied here. We document below the full adaptation choices, hyperparameters, and failure modes. 

FastV adaptation. FastV Chen et al. [2024] performs early-layer attention-based visual token pruning. 

Hyperparameters. We use the default FastV configuration from the original paper: prune ratio R =0.5 (drop 50% of image tokens) at layer K = 2 , selecting tokens by average attention weight from all text tokens to each image token across the first K layers. We also swept R в€€ { 0.25 , 0.50 , 0.75 }

and K в€€ { 2, 4, 6} per VLA and report the best SR configuration in the main table ( R=0 .5, K=2 in both cases); more aggressive pruning (e.g., R=0 .75 at K=2 ) collapsed SR to <20%.

Why SR degrades. Dual-system action heads attend densely to all VLM hidden-state positions (either the final feature grid for GR00TвЂ™s DiT head, or the per-layer KV cache for ПЂ0.5вЂ™s flow-matching expert). Zeroing out 50% of image-token positions produces sharp, structured holes in the attention map that the pretrained action head is not trained to handle. SR drops by an average of в€ј18pp; the degradation is largest on long-horizon LIBERO-10 ( в€’34pp), consistent with the action head compounding errors from these holes across the 8-step action chunks. The large drop on dual-system VLAs is thus an architectural-sensitivity finding inherent to dense-attention action heads. 

VLA-Cache adaptation. VLA-Cache Xu et al. [2025] reuses image-token KV entries across consecutive timesteps when inter-frame patch-wise visual similarity is high. 

Hyperparameters. We use the original paperвЂ™s reported hyperparameters: per-patch cosine-similarity threshold П„ = 0 .1, reuse policy вЂњcache if cosine в‰Ґ 1 в€’ П„ , else refreshвЂќ. We also swept П„ в€€{0.05 , 0.10 , 0.20 } and report the best SR ( П„ = 0 .10 ) in the main table; a tighter threshold ( П„ =0 .05 )yielded higher SR at the cost of almost no latency reduction ( <5%). 

Integration point. Identical refresh mask is applied to every transformer layerвЂ™s KV cache for image-token positions; text-token KVs are always refreshed. We do not modify the action head; it consumes the (partially cached) KV cache as-is. 

Why the speedup ceiling is low. Because VLA-Cache still runs a full forward pass through the LLM backboneвЂ”only cached-position K/V are skippedвЂ”the latency reduction is bounded by the fraction of backbone compute spent on image-token K/V projections (roughly 15вЂ“20% on both VLAs). Observed per-step latency reduction is 14вЂ“17%, yielding a net 1.08 Г— wall-clock speedup. This is the upper bound of the method on dual-system architectures; Latent Bridge exceeds it by replacing the backbone entirely on bridge steps, achieving 39вЂ“45% per-step reduction and 1.65вЂ“1.73 Г— net speedup. 

Amdahl ceiling: why token-pruning speedups shrink on dual-system VLAs. The modest net speedup of FastV and VLA-Cache in Table 1 is not an implementation artifact but a structural consequence of AmdahlвЂ™s law applied to the dual-system latency breakdown. On single-system VLAs (e.g., OpenVLA), the LLM backbone is the entire inference pipelineвЂ”the same LLM produces action tokens directlyвЂ”so any reduction in LLM latency translates 1-to-1 into end-to-end speedup. In that setting, FastV and VLA-Cache each report roughly 1.3вЂ“1.5Г— wall-clock speedup in their original papers. 16 Table 13: Cross-suite transfer of the ПЂ0.5 Spatial bridge ( f =3 ). On dual-system VLAs the same pruning only accelerates the first system (the VLM backbone), while the second system (the action head) runs unchanged at every step. Using our measured breakdown (Table 11), the backbone fraction of per-step sync cost is 63 /90 = 70% on GR00T and 46 /76 = 60%

Latent Bridge escapes this ceiling by replacing the backbone entirely on bridge steps: bridge step cost is 2вЂ“6 ms versus a 46вЂ“63 ms VLM backbone, a в‰€10 вЂ“30 Г— local speedup at the backbone-step level. This is the central architectural reason why delta prediction dominates token pruning in this setting: skipping a call is fundamentally cheaper than making an existing call faster.

## J Limitations 

State-in-prompt configurations. For VLA variants that tokenize proprioceptive state into the language prompt (e.g., ПЂ0.5 with discrete_state_input=True ), the bridgeвЂ™s cached prefix KV carries stale state when reused across bridge steps, degrading performance. In our experiments, the ALOHA ПЂ0.5 model required fine-tuning with discrete_state_input=False (state routed through the action expert instead of the language prompt) to enable bridge compatibility. This limitation is specific to state-in-prompt designs and does not affect the standard LIBERO ПЂ0.5 or GR00T configurations. 

Online DAgger requires a simulator. Stage 3 of our pipeline requires deploying the bridge in simulation to collect DAgger data. For real-robot deployment, the bridge would need to be trained entirely offline on sync data (R0), potentially with augmented data generation.
